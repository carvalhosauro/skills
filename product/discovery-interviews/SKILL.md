# Discovery Interviews

This skill serves the endpoints of the interview — the user is the one talking to real people. It acts
at two moments: **before** (preparing a guide that doesn't bias) and **after** (synthesizing what was
collected into usable signal). The goal of every discovery interview is **to validate that the pain is
real** — not to sell the idea, not to collect compliments.

## The governing principle: Mom Test

The name comes from the idea of asking questions so good that even your mom couldn't lie to please you.
Rules that apply to both preparation and synthesis:

- **Fact > opinion.** What a person *did* counts; what they *think they would do* is worthless.
  "I would buy this" is garbage. "Last month I paid $80 to an assistant to solve this" is gold.
- **Concrete past, not hypothetical future.** Ask about the last time the problem happened,
  not what they would do in an imaginary scenario.
- **Don't pitch.** The moment you describe your solution, the conversation dies and becomes polite praise.
- **Praise is not data.** "What a great idea" is a sign of failure, not validation — it means you
  pitched instead of investigating.
- **Dig.** The first answer is shallow. "Why?", "tell me about the last time", "and then what did you do?".

## Prerequisite — Wedge / Benchmark (do NOT skip)

This skill **only runs with a Benchmark ready** (which contains the wedge and the hypotheses to test).
Why: the interview exists to validate a specific hypothesis. Without the wedge, the guide becomes loose
questions and the synthesis has nothing to compare against.

**Before anything, locate the Benchmark:** check sent files and the Product OS folder (in
Claude.ai/Cowork: `/mnt/user-data/uploads/` and `/mnt/user-data/outputs/`, looking for `benchmark-*.md`).
If not found, ask the user if they have it.

**If there is no Benchmark anywhere: STOP.** Don't improvise. Immediately invoke the
`competitive-benchmark` skill using the Skill tool — it will cascade to `market-research` too if
that's also missing. Once it completes, read the output and continue.

**When it exists**, read it and extract: the wedge, positioning, target segment, and complaint patterns —
these are the **hypotheses** the interviews will test.

## Mode detection

Identify what the user wants:

- Did they bring **notes/transcripts** from interviews (pasted or in a file)? → **Synthesize mode**.
- Do they want **questions** / haven't interviewed yet? → **Prepare mode**.
- Ambiguous? Ask in one line which of the two they need.

## PREPARE mode — generate the guide

### Phase 0 — Framing (brainstorm)

From the wedge, propose (for the user to correct):

1. **Who to interview** — the exact target segment profile, and where to find them.
2. **Hypotheses to test** — 2-4 falsifiable statements extracted from the wedge. E.g.: "freelance
   nail techs lose at least 1 customer/week due to manual scheduling failures over WhatsApp".

Lock the hypotheses before writing the questions — the guide exists to test them.

### Guide structure

Build the guide in this order (broad to specific, never pitching):

1. **Warm-up / routine** — understand the person's context and day-to-day.
2. **Dig into the pain** — the last time the problem happened; what was done about it.
3. **Frequency** — how often it happens (numbers, not "sometimes").
4. **Impact** — how much it costs ($/time/lost customers/stress).
5. **How they solve it today** — the current solution, even if it's a workaround.
6. **What they've tried and paid for** — other solutions tested and discarded, money already spent.
   (This block is the best detector of real pain.)
7. **Wrap-up** — "is there anyone else I should talk to?" + let the person pull out the solution,
   if they want (you don't offer it).

For **each question**, prefer open-ended and past-focused. Include in the guide a short reminder of
**forbidden vs good** questions:

```
✗ "Don't you think a scheduling app would help?"    (biases + pitches)
✓ "Tell me about the last time a client no-showed. What happened?"

✗ "How much would you pay for this?"                (hypothetical)
✓ "What have you already spent trying to solve this?"
```

Save the guide: `interview-guide-[niche-kebab]-[YYYY-MM-DD].md`.

## SYNTHESIZE mode — analyze what was collected

### Per interview

For each interview (notes or transcript), extract:

```
Interviewee:      [profile]
Problem:          [the pain, in the person's language]
Frequency:        [how recurring — with numbers if available]
Impact:           [$ / time / consequence]
Who suffers:      [is this person the one who suffers? who pays? who decides?]
How they solve it today: [current solution / workaround]
Already tried/paid: [tested solutions, money spent]
Quotes:           [literal excerpts — preserve their words]
Signal:           [strong / weak — see rule below]
```

**Signal rule (Mom Test applied):**
- **Strong** = past behavior, money/time already spent, a workaround they built, genuine emotion when
  describing the problem, asked to be notified when a solution exists.
- **Weak** = compliments ("great idea"), hypotheticals ("I would use it"), enthusiasm with no past action.
  Discount praise — it falsely inflates validation.

### In aggregate

```
## Aggregate synthesis
Interviews analyzed:  [n]
Hypothesis tested:    [from the wedge]

Patterns:             [what repeats across interviews]
Strong signals:       [how many/which ones]
Surprises/divergences:[what contradicted the expectation]

Read:                 [validated / partial / refuted] — [why in 1-2 sentences]
Next step:            [more interviews? reformulate the problem? kill?]
```

Save: `interview-synthesis-[niche-kebab]-[YYYY-MM-DD].md`. In chat, give just the read + strong signals.

## Pitfalls (where this usually fails)

- **Concluding "validated" with 2-3 enthusiastic interviews** → enthusiasm is not purchase. Require a
  past behavior signal and a reasonable minimum sample before declaring validation.
- **Treating praise as data** → in synthesis, compliments always count as weak signal.
- **A guide that pitches or biases** → every question must be answerable without the person knowing
  which answer you want to hear.
- **Asking about the future** → "would you use/pay?" doesn't predict behavior. Anchor to the past.
- **Only talking to easy people** (friends, same bubble) → biases toward praise. Remind the user to
  seek the real segment.
- **Losing the customer's language** → keep the literal quotes; they become the product text and the
  Problem Formulation afterward.
