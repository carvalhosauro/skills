# Competitive Benchmark

This skill understands **what already exists** to find **where to enter**. The goal is not to catalog
competitor features (that becomes a generic roadmap copied from the competition) — it's to find the
**gap**: the intersection of an underserved pain, a neglected segment, and something that can be done
differently. That gap is the *wedge*: the narrow crack through which a small product enters.

The output is a **comparison matrix**, a gap analysis, and a wedge recommendation with suggested
positioning — all saved as a `.md` file in the Product OS.

---

## Prerequisite — Market Map (do NOT skip this check)

This skill **only runs with a Market Map ready**. Why: benchmarking without a defined market becomes
a feature list without criteria — you don't know which features matter because you don't know who the
customer is or what hurts. The Market Map provides the niche, segments, pains, and initial competitors
that give the comparison meaning.

**Before anything else, locate the Market Map:**

1. Check files sent by the user and the Product OS output folder
   (in Claude.ai/Cowork: `/mnt/user-data/uploads/` and `/mnt/user-data/outputs/`, looking for
   `market-map-*.md`).
2. If not found, ask the user if they have the Map (they can paste it or send the file).

**If there is no Market Map anywhere: STOP.** Don't improvise the market research here.
Immediately invoke the `market-research` skill using the Skill tool — it generates the Market Map
automatically. Once it completes, read the output file and continue with Phase 0 below.

**When the Map exists**, read it and extract: the scope (product + niche), segments, prioritized pains,
already-cited competitors, and the unknowns raised. All of this is the starting point.

---

## Phase 0 — Framing (brainstorm with the user)

With the Map in hand, define **who** goes into the analysis and **what criteria** to compare. As in
the other skills, propose a first cut and let the user correct — don't ask blank questions.

1. **Competitor list.** Start from the ones in the Map. Propose completing with (a) direct competitors
   that are missing and (b) **indirect/manual alternatives** (spreadsheet, WhatsApp, notebook, "do nothing").
   The manual alternative is almost always the real competitor to beat — don't ignore it.
   Aim for 3 to 7 competitors; more than that dilutes the analysis.
2. **Comparison criteria.** Propose criteria that matter **for that specific niche**, not generic ones.
   For nail tech scheduling, for example: WhatsApp integration, automatic reminders, shareable booking link,
   advance payment, price, learning curve. Generic criteria ("has a dashboard?") produce a useless matrix.

Lock the framing before continuing:

```
## Benchmark scope
Competitors:  [3-7, including the manual alternative]
Criteria:     [niche-specific]
```

---

## Phase 1 — Collection per competitor

**Check if web search is available.** With search, investigate live; without it, do the best with your
own knowledge and warn at the top of the report that the data needs validation.

For **each competitor**, gather (this is the per-competitor skeleton):

- **Audience** — who they actually sell to (may differ from what they say they sell to).
- **Price / model** — ranges, billing model, free plan.
- **Features** — fill in against the Phase 0 criteria, not a loose list.
- **Differentiators** — what they use to stand out; their positioning/messaging.
- **Real customer complaints** — **the gold of the benchmark.** Go to reviews (G2, Capterra, app stores),
  Reddit, threads. Recurring complaints reveal the gap: what customers pay for and still hate is your
  entry point. Use the customer's literal language.

---

## Phase 2 — Synthesis (matrix + gaps + wedge)

### 1. Comparison matrix

Criteria in rows, competitors in columns (include the manual alternative). Make the gap visible —
use simple markers (✓ / partial / ✗) and be honest about what nobody does well.

```markdown
| Criterion           | Comp. A | Comp. B | Comp. C | Manual alternative |
|---------------------|:-------:|:-------:|:-------:|:-----------------:|
| [niche criterion]   |    ✓    | partial |    ✗    |         ✗         |
```

### 2. Gap analysis

- **Table stakes** — what *everyone* has. Mandatory; not a differentiator, it's the cost of entry.
- **Where nobody does well** — criteria where most are ✗ or "partial". Gap candidates.
- **Complaint patterns** — the same pain cited across multiple competitors = clear opportunity.

### 3. Wedge recommendation

Point to **one** wedge: the intersection of (underserved pain) × (segment neglected by leaders) ×
(something a small product can do better or simpler). Resist the temptation to "do everything better" —
a real wedge is **narrow and specific**. Deliver:

- **Wedge:** the crack, in one sentence.
- **Suggested positioning:** "[product] for [segment] who [pain], different because [unique axis]."
- **What to deliberately NOT do** (at least at first) — just as important as what to do.

### Report template (use this exact structure)

```markdown
# Competitive Benchmark — [niche]
> Based on: [Market Map file] · Generated on [date] · [with / without] live search

## Summary
[3-5 sentences: competitive landscape + the main gap in one line.]

## Comparison matrix
[criteria × competitors table]

## Competitor profiles
[Per competitor: audience, price, differentiators, and — highlighted — real complaints.]

## Gap analysis
**Table stakes:** [...]
**Where nobody does well:** [...]
**Complaint patterns:** [...]

## Recommended wedge
**Wedge:** [one sentence]
**Positioning:** [product] for [segment] who [pain], different because [axis].
**What NOT to do at first:** [...]

## Next step
[Which wedge hypotheses need to become interview questions / problem formulation.]
```

## Pitfalls (where this usually fails)

- **Improvising research when the Map is missing** → is exactly what this skill must NOT do.
  Redirect to market-research.
- **Copying the competitor feature list as a roadmap** → is the opposite of the wedge. The wedge often
  means doing *less*, but better, for a specific segment.
- **Generic criteria** → a matrix that distinguishes nobody. Criteria must be niche-specific.
- **Ignoring the manual alternative** → the most common competitor (WhatsApp/spreadsheet) is left out
  and the analysis loses its real customer reference.
- **Confusing table stakes with differentiators** → leads you to invest where it's already a commodity.
- **Wide wedge ("be better at everything")** → not a wedge, it's small-product suicide. Force the
  recommendation to be narrow.
- **Under-using complaints** → they're the richest source of gaps. If the collection has no real
  complaints, the analysis is weak.
